Diagnostic Strategy, Test Selection & Laboratory Stewardship

Clinical Parasitology and Mycology · Module 3 · Master of Science in Medical Laboratory Sciences

A correct diagnostic pathway begins before the first result exists.

This module develops strategic diagnostic reasoning from the laboratory perspective. The focus is not how to perform the test or how to interpret a completed result alone. The focus is how to choose the right specimen, the right first-line test, the right escalation pathway, and the right level of diagnostic complexity for the scenario in front of you.

Overview

Why diagnostic strategy matters as much as method knowledge and interpretation.

Anchor idea: a sophisticated laboratory is not defined by how many tests it can run, but by how well it knows which test should be run first, when a specimen is unsuitable, when escalation is justified, and when high-sensitivity testing may create more noise than clarity.
Why this module matters

Wrong strategy can distort the entire diagnostic process. A poor specimen sent to a high-end assay may still mislead. A broad multiplex panel ordered without a clear diagnostic question may generate results of uncertain significance. A delayed or inappropriate specimen can reduce the value of even a well-chosen method.

  • Wrong first test can create false reassurance.
  • Over-testing can generate low-significance positives.
  • Poor timing can make a biologically real process invisible.
What you are strengthening
  • Defining the diagnostic question before ordering a method.
  • Choosing specimens that match the biology of the target and the strengths of the assay.
  • Balancing turnaround time, yield, cost, and practicality.
  • Escalating from routine to advanced testing only when justified.
  • Recognizing when a powerful test is the wrong first choice.
How this differs from Modules 1 and 2

Module 1 focused on methods. Module 2 focused on result meaning. Module 3 focuses on strategy.

  • Not “How does PCR work?”
  • Not “How should this PCR result be reported?”
  • But “Should PCR be ordered at this stage, on this specimen, for this question?”

Diagnostic Strategy Framework

A structured laboratory approach to designing the diagnostic pathway before testing begins.

What exactly are you trying to detect: organism, syndrome, marker, or viability?

2

Which specimen best matches the biology of the target and the method?

3

What is the most appropriate initial test at this stage?

4
Escalation

When is confirmatory or advanced testing justified?

5
Constraints

What do cost, availability, and turnaround time do to the pathway?

6
Stewardship

Does this plan maximize diagnostic value without unnecessary testing?

Step 1 · Define the question
  • Do you need broad syndrome screening or targeted confirmation?
  • Are you looking for whole organisms, antigens, nucleic acid, or viable recovery?
  • Would a negative result from the chosen method genuinely answer the question?
Step 2 · Match the specimen
  • Serial stool specimens increase parasitology yield where shedding is intermittent.
  • Respiratory source quality changes the meaning of fungal recovery.
  • Non-sterile specimens should not be treated like sterile-site evidence.
Step 3 onward · Sequence wisely
  • Use a routine, affordable first step when appropriate.
  • Escalate to antigen or molecular when the question remains unresolved.
  • Reserve broad or high-cost methods for justified scenarios.
Strategy rule: the best test is not the newest test; it is the test that best fits the diagnostic question, the specimen, and the context of use.

Specimen & Timing

Specimen quality and timing are not minor details; they determine whether the pathway has diagnostic value.

Specimen suitability
  • Stool parasitology: one specimen can miss intermittent shedding; serial specimens improve sensitivity.
  • Respiratory mycology: sputum quality strongly influences significance of growth.
  • Urine mycology: contamination and colonization issues make source interpretation difficult.
  • Tissue / sterile fluid: often provides stronger significance than non-sterile sources.
Timing
  • Low organism burden early in the process can make microscopy falsely reassuring.
  • Timing affects antigen yield and molecular detectability.
  • Repeat sampling may be more valuable than immediate blind escalation in some scenarios.

Test Selection Logic

Choose the method that answers the question best, not simply the method with the highest technological appeal.

Targeted vs broad testing

Broad testing is useful when the syndrome is unclear, but it should not replace a defined diagnostic question when a focused pathway is more efficient and interpretable.

Screening vs diagnostic use

A method that works well in a symptomatic, high-probability setting may behave very differently in low-context screening.

Sensitivity vs meaning

The most sensitive test may create the most interpretation burden if the specimen and clinical question are poorly aligned.

Laboratory Stewardship

Stewardship in the diagnostic laboratory means choosing a pathway that is justified, proportionate, and high-yield rather than automatically choosing the broadest, most expensive, or most technologically advanced option.

Core principle: more testing does not automatically improve diagnosis. Unfocused escalation can increase cost, generate low-significance positives, complicate reporting, and delay a cleaner answer that might have been reached through better specimen strategy or a more targeted first step.
Why stewardship matters

Every diagnostic pathway carries a cost: financial cost, time cost, interpretive cost, and sometimes even credibility cost if the laboratory generates technically correct but poorly directed results.

  • Over-testing increases the chance of low-value positives.
  • Poor sequencing can bypass simpler, higher-yield steps.
  • Unfocused escalation can make reporting more difficult rather than more meaningful.

Strong stewardship protects the quality of laboratory reasoning, not just the budget.

Over-testing and overdiagnosis

High-sensitivity methods can identify targets that are real at the analytical level but weak in diagnostic significance.

  • Broad multiplex testing may detect organisms of uncertain relevance when the diagnostic question is poorly defined.
  • Repeated biomarker testing without a clear purpose can create interpretive noise.
  • Molecular escalation on a weak specimen may amplify low-value information rather than solve the problem.

Stewardship message: the laboratory should not confuse detectability with usefulness.

Cost-effectiveness and proportionality

A good pathway is not necessarily the cheapest pathway, but it should be proportionate to the question being asked.

  • Use lower-cost, lower-complexity steps when they can answer the question adequately.
  • Escalate when the question remains unresolved, not by routine habit.
  • Reserve advanced assays for situations where they meaningfully improve yield, speed, or discriminatory power.

Best practice: choose the level of testing that fits the level of uncertainty.

Common stewardship failures
  • Ordering a broad panel before defining the actual diagnostic target.
  • Escalating to advanced testing despite poor specimen quality.
  • Repeating the same test without asking whether repeat sampling or a better specimen would help more.
  • Using biomarkers outside the settings where they add strong value.
  • Choosing the most advanced test because it is available rather than because it is appropriate.
Stewardship mindset for MLS practice
  • Ask: What question am I trying to answer?
  • Ask: Is this the best specimen for that question?
  • Ask: Would a better specimen add more value than a more complex assay?
  • Ask: If this test is positive, will it truly clarify the pathway?
  • Ask: If this test is negative, will it meaningfully reduce uncertainty?

Strategic rule: stewardship is not about doing less; it is about doing what is justified, timed correctly, and likely to add meaningful diagnostic value.

Diagnostic Algorithms

Structured pathways reduce random ordering and improve consistency.

Algorithm 1 · Chronic diarrheal work-up

A strategy pathway from low-cost first-line testing to escalation.

Step 1: Begin with serial stool specimens rather than a single specimen when parasitology yield matters.

Rationale: repeated stool sampling improves detection where shedding is intermittent.

Step 2: If microscopy remains negative but the diagnostic question persists, move to targeted antigen or molecular testing rather than restating the same low-yield approach.

Rationale: escalation should be justified by unresolved question, not by habit.

Algorithm 2 · Suspected invasive fungal disease

A strategy pathway emphasizing specimen strength and context-aware escalation.

Step 1: Assess the strongest available specimen and whether a non-sterile respiratory source will produce meaningful information.

Rationale: source quality changes the value of fungal recovery.

Step 2: Use biomarkers and molecular escalation strategically, not reflexively, and interpret them in their intended context.

Rationale: supportive tests are valuable when the scenario justifies them.

Cheat Sheet

High-yield strategy rules for choosing tests and designing pathways.

Core strategy rules
First question
Define what you are trying to detect before choosing the method.
Specimen logic
A wrong specimen weakens even a strong assay.
Escalation
Move from routine to advanced testing only when the question remains unresolved.
Stewardship
The best pathway is high-yield, proportional, and interpretable.
Common traps
Trap 1
Using the newest test as the first test without asking whether it is the right test.
Trap 2
Ignoring serial sampling when biology makes one specimen low yield.
Trap 3
Ordering broad panels without a clear diagnostic question.
Trap 4
Escalating complexity when improving the specimen would be more valuable.
Decision triggers
Repeat specimen
When biology, timing, or source quality limits initial yield.
Targeted antigen
When a focused rapid question remains after routine steps.
Targeted PCR
When higher sensitivity or discriminatory power is justified.
Broad panel
When syndrome coverage adds value and is not likely to create mostly low-value positives.
Best mindset
Do not ask
“What is the most advanced test?”
Ask instead
“What test best answers the current question with the current specimen?”
Keep in mind
Cost, speed, specimen strength, and interpretability.
Final goal
A pathway that is rational, defensible, and high-yield.
