The goal is not just to produce a result, but to produce a result that is defensible.

Overview

Why quality is a laboratory system, not a final step at the end of testing.

Anchor idea: a result can be analytically generated and still be untrustworthy. Quality in parasitology and mycology depends on the full chain: correct specimen, correct handling, correct controls, correct run acceptance, correct verification, and correct reporting.
What this module covers
  • Daily quality control versus system-level quality assurance.
  • Pre-analytical failures such as labeling, timing, and specimen suitability.
  • Analytical failures such as contamination, control failure, and weak reproducibility.
  • Post-analytical failures such as unsafe release, missing qualifiers, and inconsistent comments.
  • Root cause analysis and prevention of recurrence.
What makes this non-redundant

Module 1 explained methods. Module 2 explained meaning. Module 3 explained strategy. Module 4 explains reliability.

  • Not how a PCR works.
  • Not how to interpret a PCR result.
  • Not when to choose PCR.
  • But how to know whether the PCR run was valid, controlled, documented, and safe to release.

Quality Assurance vs Quality Control

QC checks today’s run. QA protects the whole laboratory system.

1
QC

Controls, stain checks, reagent checks, run acceptance.

2
QA

SOPs, training, documentation, audits, proficiency testing.

3
QC failure

Means this run may be invalid now.

4
QA failure

Means the system allows the problem to recur.

5
Corrective action

Fix today’s failure.

6
Preventive action

Protect tomorrow’s process.

Quality control in actual practice
  • Positive and negative controls in serology and molecular runs.
  • Stain quality checks before relying on morphology.
  • Media and reagent checks before culture or antigen work.
  • Run rejection if control performance fails.
Quality assurance in actual practice
  • Standardized SOPs for sample receipt, processing, and release.
  • Competency assessment and retraining.
  • Documentation, audit trail, and occurrence logging.
  • External quality assessment and inter-laboratory comparison.

Pre-Analytical Quality

The quality of the result begins before the test starts.

Highest-risk zone: labeling, collection, transport, preservation, and specimen adequacy failures can invalidate the entire pathway before the assay even begins.
Specimen identity and integrity
  • Correct patient/sample matching.
  • Leakage, breakage, or incorrect container problems.
  • Specimen rejection criteria must be clear and applied consistently.
Timing and preservation
  • Delay can alter morphology and reduce interpretability.
  • Serial stool strategy matters when shedding is intermittent.
  • Poor transport conditions can compromise organisms and markers.
Source suitability
  • Poor respiratory specimens weaken mycology significance.
  • Non-sterile source results need source-aware caution.
  • A better specimen may add more value than a more complex assay.

Analytical Quality

Even a good specimen can produce poor-quality data if the run is not controlled.

Daily analytical control points
  • Positive and negative control performance.
  • Reagent integrity and lot consistency.
  • Contamination control in molecular work.
  • Inter-observer consistency in microscopy.
  • Instrument/platform suitability for the target organism.
Typical analytical failures
  • Positive negative-control in PCR.
  • Weak or inconsistent staining causing morphology uncertainty.
  • Culture contamination mistaken for genuine growth.
  • Premature final identification from an unsupported workflow.

Post-Analytical Quality

Results must be verified for accuracy, consistency, and safe communication before release.

Verification before release
  • Does the result match what the method can truly support?
  • Does the report need a limitation comment?
  • Has discordance across methods been flagged?
Common release failures
  • Definitive wording from a limited method.
  • Missing qualifiers when source quality is weak.
  • Inconsistent language across staff for the same finding.
Professional release rule

A result should be released only when the laboratory can defend the specimen, the run, the wording, and the consistency of its process.

Root Cause Analysis

System-level reasoning using real laboratory failures. Each cause is expanded through practice-grounded examples.

Key principle: Root cause analysis is not about identifying what went wrong — it is about identifying why the system allowed it to happen.
What RCA asks
  • What failed first?
  • What allowed the failure to continue?
  • What part of the system was missing or weak?

RCA should move from the visible error to the system condition underneath it.

Corrective vs preventive action
  • Corrective action protects the current case or run.
  • Preventive action changes the process so the same weakness is less likely to recur.

Strong RCA always includes both.

How to read the cards
  • Front: what the bench staff notices first.
  • Back: what the deeper system problem actually is.

This mirrors real quality review: observation first, root cause second.

Human factor

Operator-related error

Surface issue: Incorrect pipetting, misreading microscopy, or premature reporting.

What you observe
  • Inconsistent results across staff
  • Unexpected discrepancy in repeated tests
Hidden risk

The system depends too heavily on individual performance rather than structured reliability.

Flip to analyze deeper cause
What actually caused it?

Not simply “human error”. Usually linked to:

  • Inadequate competency assessment
  • Fatigue or workload pressure
  • Unclear SOPs or inconsistent training
Real example

WHO LQMS reports show misidentification in microscopy often arises from lack of periodic competency checks rather than individual negligence.

Prevention
  • Structured competency programs
  • Standardized reporting templates
  • Double reading in high-risk cases
Process failure

Weak or missing SOP

Surface issue: Different staff performing the same test differently.

What you observe
  • Variation in reporting language
  • Different interpretations for identical findings
Hidden risk

Lack of standardization leads to unreliable laboratory output.

Flip to analyze deeper cause
What actually caused it?

Process inconsistency due to:

  • Outdated or unclear SOP
  • Lack of standardization
  • No audit or enforcement
Real example

CLSI highlights variability in fungal reporting when laboratories lack standardized interpretive comments.

Prevention
  • Clear SOPs with version control
  • Regular audits
  • Mandatory adherence monitoring
Equipment

Instrument or reagent issue

Surface issue: Unexpected control failure or inconsistent results.

What you observe
  • Control behaving abnormally
  • Drift in expected assay performance
Hidden risk

The analytical system may be compromised even if results appear plausible.

Flip to analyze deeper cause
What actually caused it?

Often linked to:

  • Expired or degraded reagents
  • Poor calibration
  • Environmental contamination
Real example

CDC molecular diagnostics guidance shows PCR contamination events frequently arise from poor workflow separation rather than assay failure.

Prevention
  • Routine calibration and maintenance
  • Strict contamination control zones
  • Reagent tracking and validation
Specimen

Specimen-related issue

Surface issue: False negatives or misleading results from poor samples.

What you observe
  • Low organism yield
  • Inconsistent findings across samples
Hidden risk

The problem lies in input quality, not analytical performance.

Flip to analyze deeper cause
What actually caused it?
  • Incorrect collection technique
  • Delayed transport
  • Inappropriate specimen type
Real example

CDC DPDx emphasizes that single stool samples can miss intermittent parasite shedding, leading to false reassurance.

Prevention
  • Clear collection instructions
  • Rejection criteria
  • Serial sampling protocols
Communication

Reporting failure

Surface issue: Results issued without appropriate qualifiers or consistency.

What you observe
  • Overconfident wording
  • Missing limitations or disclaimers
Hidden risk

The laboratory output may misrepresent the strength of evidence.

Flip to analyze deeper cause
What actually caused it?
  • Lack of reporting standards
  • Inconsistent training
  • Absence of verification step
Real example

IDSA guidelines emphasize cautious wording in fungal biomarkers to avoid overstatement of significance.

Prevention
  • Standardized reporting language
  • Mandatory verification before release
  • Periodic report audits

Read the bench context

Classify the failure

Think like a quality lead

After identifying the error, ask what must be corrected now and what must change in the system to prevent repetition.

Cheat Sheet

High-yield reliability rules for bench practice and result release.

QC vs QA
QC
Checks whether today’s run or process is acceptable.
QA
Protects the whole system through SOPs, documentation, training, and assessment.
Corrective action
Fixes today’s problem.
Preventive action
Reduces the chance of recurrence.
Release rules
Never release
Results from an invalid run or failed control.
Pause release
When discordance, unsupported wording, or specimen quality issues remain unresolved.
Verify
Specimen, run, wording, and consistency before final issue.
Root cause prompts
What happened?
Describe the event clearly.
Why did it happen?
Look beyond the visible mistake.

Protect current patient results immediately.

Update the system, not just the moment.
